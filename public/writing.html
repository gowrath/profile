Maps and the World

In my last post, I mentioned the importance of stories—or at least how paying attention to the wrong metric might lead an organization astray.

In this post, I want to explore two things:

    1) Recency bias
    2) The Map and Territory relationship

As a former teacher, I constantly dealt with the common problem of executing lesson plans in a way that increased student understanding and engaged everyone in the class. I did have 150 students in my caseload, which made that difficult, but teachers are held to impossible standards ipso facto. Good teachers can shift lessons on the fly, subtly or substantially. To nix certain activities and to emphasize other ones. To pause in the middle of lessons to delve into points and to ask students to confirm their understanding.

In this vein, it is intellectually humbling when a lesson falls flat or you realize that your perfectly designed lesson doesn’t perform as well in real-life. Whatever the curriculum design, teachers know that how students receive the information is ultimately the key differentiator in acquiring knowledge. Sometimes the best-designed/intentioned lesson goes over students’ heads. At times, an activity that you spent 10 minutes creating is a hit in class, providing a rich educational experience. This dichotomy between theory and real-life performance is something that fascinates me.

How does this relate to metrics and recency bias? Recency bias—the excessive focus on the most recent events or facts—pervades business thinking today. It’s a what did you do for me lately attitude. What were your last quarter's sales? What’s your forecast? What was your last valuation? And for good reason—investors are skeptical and will shift capital to avoid opportunity cost and maximize return. Yet, the most successful businesses today are those with long-term views. They are in every industry and have consistent themes of business practices.

I want to merge the two ideas—the territory and map relation and recency bias. On a practical level, what happens when the territory changes and the map does not? When the underlying reality has changed but the metric has not? To take a step further, what if the attempts to solve this problem discount historical events? If short-term fixes don’t address the underlying problem (and world) that has changed underneath your feet?

I can only speak to my educational experience to answer these questions, but I think you should constantly question your worldview and pursue the truth—honest, unfiltered, with fidelity to the underlying history and present-day facts. Truth, or at least knowledge, is social. It’s created between people who engage in purposeful knowledge creation. Teachers stitch their maps of knowledge with their students, and people create quilts of understanding, and that quilt gets melded into society as a whole.

Surrogation and Learning

As I work in instructional design, I’m constantly surrounded by similar problems I encountered during my four years of teaching. One is addressing learning gaps. Another is creating assessments based on what you actually teach.   

I want to think through some of the standards and metrics that businesses measure. An interesting point by Bezos stirred my thinking: https://youtu.be/pyyP73xc7bQ?si=SJclvh1JiPaBEdV5&t=210  

One great insight of this talk is a skeptical view of proxies–meaning that you are interested in what the metric measures, not the metric itself.   

Two interesting articles here:   

https://hbr.org/2019/09/dont-let-metrics-undermine-your-business   https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surrogation  

The first one explains how focus on a single metric can be counterproductive to ethical (and ultimately reputational) business practice, and the second one focuses on how adherence to a score such as customer satisfaction (like CSAT) can distort your understanding of the underlying phenomenon.   

How does this relate to instructional design? At times, we read feedback that either confirms that this is good learning (high NPS/CSAT scores), but we forget that the learner experience is what we are trying to capture.   

According to the Journal of Accounting Research, “an everyday example of surrogation is a manager tasked with increasing customer satisfaction who begins to believe that the customer satisfaction survey score actually is customer satisfaction.”  

Teachers are very aware of this dynamic because we face it every single day. We assess students—we figure out if they’re understanding, engaging, and writing cogently. But one single writing assessment—or even a reading score such as SRI—is simply a measure of reading skill. It is useful in so far as it actually diagnoses the gaps in learning. In so far as what specific language skills that the student needs to focus on.   

I encountered this recently as I am working on a course and receiving feedback on it. There are topics that students need—how to debug, for example. This is a crucial skill that we are not teaching currently. And yet–it’s not being captured in our scores of satisfaction. This metric isn’t evaluating the learner experience. Or in this case, the developer experience.   

And it’s here I’m humbled by my teaching experience because I realize that we are accessing students on a skill (building an app) that requires other skills that we have not explicitly taught them. (I am thinking about making a video on debugging to help develop this skill.) But it pairs with the idea that you should always be on guard for how that metric is accurately capturing reality.  

As another example, I faced this when I administered standardized tests in my classroom, and I was frustrated with regard to giving students a score without explaining what that score meant, and why it was important (it wasn’t). The reality is that the learner experience is paramount, in education and in corporate training. You cannot manage to irrelevant metrics in a world that is changing by the day. Perhaps the SAT really does skew to higher income folks who understand the word “equestrian” and specific cultural references. Perhaps stock price shouldn’t be a proxy for business success. Perhaps quarterly earnings shouldn’t be the most salient way that companies should be measured. You get my point.  

What my ultimate thesis here is that we need a tighter adherence to the underlying truth of the learning process. We need precise data. We need anecdotes. We need a skeptical view of metrics. We actually need stories—ones in which blinding following a metric led people astray.  

These lessons will clarify our understanding of the world. We will teach exactly what students need to know and their understanding will reflect our understanding. It will be a 1:1; it will not require guesswork and individual troubleshooting.  

This focus on learning objectives and teaching to them will, in my estimation, improve the functioning of organizations today. We will clear up some of the sloppy thinking that permeates business practice. We will start to see the world clearer and sweep away some of the mysticism (what amounts to wishful thinking) around how to measure customer satisfaction.

Learning, long-term thinking

Thoughts

My thoughts around learning in the workplace has a lot to do with my teaching experience. I worked with primarily 8th, 9th, and 12th graders, and I got a firsthand insight into how they construct and apply knowledge.

My purpose in the world, at least professionally, is to carry that passion I had for teaching into great learning programs for companies.

This has been solidified this past year as I’ve become a student of data science and machine learning, staying in awe of the mathematics that undergire LLMs, etc.

I feel empathy for the learner, and find joyous occasions when I feel I’ve learned something, or finished a course.

It really makes me think of my students and how important self-concept and self-efficacy is in the context of learning. I feel like growth mindset has made its rounds in corporate parlance, but what I want to discuss is reflection and how you see yourself.

Are you a static or malleable person? Do you have the capacity to change? Most importantly, do you want to change?

I want to say that we need to create a culture shift in which learning is celebrated every single day at work, creating a virtuous cycle of knowledge sharing and collaboration.

Ironically, the pursuit of short term profits tends to deter this goal, because you tend to have a shorter view of people when you lay off and fire in hard times. When you incorrectly diagnose someone’s strengths and weaknesses.

But I harken back to Steve Jobs who seemed fairly brutal at times. But he had a great response here: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=1lEnMmH9qh4

Patience wins out. Long term goals and a clear direction of getting there. Business objectives that are longer than 3-4 quarters from now. And a clear focus on people.

I envision the workplace of the future, which actually sort of looks like my old classroom. I saw the student five years from now. I saw the student ten years from my teaching her. I saw the person that they could develop into.

I saw potential where others might find weakness. And it is my hope, as an instructional designer, to develop that potential into greatness. To transform knowledge. To make that knowledge useful. To turn that use into purpose, which is to make learning a permanent part of us.

Let’s hope we all have the same view.
